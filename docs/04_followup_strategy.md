# 5 Follow-Up Questions + Upgrade Path

> **The premise:** Before you build a strategy, you need to build a decision tree. These five questions are not intake questions — they are binary forks that determine whether you spend the next 30 days on debt brokerage pitch decks or equity underwriting models, whether you build from scratch or start with templates, and whether you focus on GitHub credibility or LinkedIn positioning. The answers change everything downstream.

---

## The 5 Strategic Questions

---

### Question 1: What's Your Target Role — Debt Brokerage, Acquisitions, or Underwriting?

**Why it matters:** These three paths have the same vocabulary but completely different day-to-day skill requirements. Conflating them is the most common strategic mistake candidates make, and it leads to interview preparation that doesn't match the role.

| Role | Primary Deliverable | Core Skill | Interview Test |
|------|--------------------|-----------|-|
| **Debt Brokerage Analyst** | Offering Memorandum + Lender Quote Matrix | Deal storytelling + lender relationship management | "Write an Investment Highlights section for this property" |
| **Acquisitions Analyst** | Acquisition model + Investment Committee memo | Deep underwriting + IRR/equity multiple analysis | "Build a 10-year DCF on this multifamily deal and tell me your recommended bid" |
| **Underwriting Analyst** (at a lender) | Credit memo + loan sizing | Credit risk assessment + debt structuring | "What's the maximum loan on this deal and what's your credit concern?" |

**What your answer unlocks:**

- **Debt Brokerage** → Focus your prep on OM writing, lender strategy, quote comparison matrices, and client communication. Your Python automation skills are a massive differentiator here — almost no other analyst candidates have them.

- **Acquisitions** → Focus on IRR/equity multiple modeling, sponsor return analysis, and waterfall structures. You'll need a deeper Excel model that includes levered returns, not just NOI and debt sizing.

- **Underwriting (lender-side)** → Focus on credit memos, covenant analysis, stress-testing, and the lender's perspective on risk. Your commercial lending background at Genpact is your primary credential here.

**If you're unsure:** Default to **Debt Brokerage** first. It has the most job openings at the analyst level, it plays most directly to your skill set (modeling + writing + client management), and it positions you to transition into acquisitions or lending later with direct deal experience.

### Question 2: Are You More Comfortable Building Excel CRE Models From Scratch, or Do You Want to Start With Templates?

**Why it matters:** This is a self-assessment question with strategic implications. The honest answer determines whether your first 30 days should be structured around building core competency or producing portfolio-quality output.

**The two paths:**

**Path A — Build From Scratch (Recommended if you have Excel modeling confidence)**

Start with a blank sheet. Column A: row labels (GPR, vacancy, EGI, OpEx line items, NOI). Dynamic assumption inputs at the top. The first model will be ugly. Build it anyway.

Why this matters: Every serious Capital Markets interview includes some version of "walk me through how you'd build this model." If you've built it from scratch, you can answer this fluently. If you've only used templates, your answer will have gaps that experienced interviewers detect immediately.

**Path B — Templates First (If CRE modeling is new territory)**

Download 2–3 professional templates:
- CBRE/JLL published deal models (ask for them via their research desks)
- LoopNet sample models
- BiggerPockets commercial model templates

Study the structure first. Understand why every row exists. Then rebuild it from a blank sheet with the template as your reference. This two-step approach gets you to "built from scratch" fluency faster than starting cold.

**What your answer unlocks:**

- If you're a strong Excel modeler: Week 1 is "build 3 models from scratch across 3 asset types." You'll be interview-ready in 2 weeks.
- If you need template scaffolding first: Week 1 is "dissect 2 templates and rebuild one." You'll be interview-ready in 3–4 weeks.

**The honest truth:** Most "experienced" candidates at the analyst level have only ever used templates. If you can build from scratch, you immediately separate yourself from 80% of the competition.

### Question 3: How Many Hours Per Day Can You Dedicate for the Next 30 Days?

**Why it matters:** This question forces a realistic capacity commitment, which determines how ambitious your 30-day plan can be and which gaps you can realistically close before you start applying.

**The three scenarios:**

| Daily Hours | Realistic Outcome at 30 Days | Priority Allocation |
|-------------|-----------------------------|-|
| **1–2 hours** | CRE vocabulary fluent, one deal model built, interview language prepared | 60% modeling, 20% vocabulary, 20% interview prep |
| **3–4 hours** | Full model + OM draft + lender matrix + 5 mock deals analyzed | 40% modeling, 25% OM/writing, 20% lender strategy, 15% Python |
| **5+ hours** | Complete portfolio: 3 deals modeled, 2 OMs written, Python tracker built, GitHub live | Full 7-step system execution |

**What your answer unlocks:**

- **Low bandwidth (1–2 hrs):** Concentrate exclusively on the three things that show up in every interview: debt sizing math (fluency from memory), deal model structure (can explain from first principles), and the answer to "walk me through how you'd underwrite this deal." Everything else is secondary.

- **Medium bandwidth (3–4 hrs):** You can close all RED gaps AND build a credible portfolio piece. This is the sweet spot — enough time to produce quality output, realistic enough to maintain over 30 days.

- **High bandwidth (5+ hrs):** Treat this like an internship prep sprint. Build real output that goes on GitHub and gets shared with recruiters. This is the path to standing out in a competitive cohort.

**The scheduling insight:** Mornings are better than evenings for model-building — analytical work requires focus that degrades after a full workday. Even 45 focused morning minutes beats 2 scattered evening hours.

### Question 4: Do You Want to Simulate a Real NYC Deal Case Study?

**Why it matters:** There is a significant difference between knowing Capital Markets concepts and being able to execute on a real deal under pressure. A simulated case study — using an actual NYC property, real market data, and realistic deal parameters — exposes every gap that abstract learning misses.

**What a real NYC deal simulation involves:**

1. **Property selection:** Choose a real asset (e.g., a 75-unit multifamily building in the Bronx listed on LoopNet at $12M, T-12 NOI of $620,000)
2. **Market research:** Pull actual submarket vacancy rates, rent comps, cap rate comps from CBRE/JLL/Cushman research reports (publicly available)
3. **Model build:** GPR → EGI → NOI from a sample rent roll, then full debt sizing at bank/debt fund/agency
4. **OM writing:** Write the Investment Highlights and Debt Opportunity sections for this specific property
5. **Lender strategy:** Choose the best lender type and explain the recommendation in a 1-paragraph deal summary
6. **Stress test:** What happens to the deal if NOI drops 15%? Which lender covenant breaks first?

**Why NYC specifically:** NYC is the single most scrutinized CRE market in the world. If you can speak intelligently about NYC deals — cap rates by borough, rent stabilization implications, debt market appetite for NYC multifamily — you can speak intelligently about any market. NYC deal knowledge signals sophistication.

**What your answer unlocks:**

- **Yes:** Move directly to a structured simulation framework. The learning from one complete simulated deal is worth 20 hours of abstract study. This is the highest-ROI activity available.
- **No / not yet:** Continue with model-building practice on generic deal structures until comfortable. But set a deadline for doing the simulation — it should happen before your first real application.

**The interview advantage:** After completing a simulated deal, your interview answer to "tell me about a deal you've analyzed" is no longer hypothetical. You have a specific property, specific numbers, and a specific recommendation. This is a transformative shift in how you come across.

### Question 5: GitHub + Portfolio Strategy for Recruiters?

**Why it matters:** In 2024–2025, a GitHub profile with CRE-relevant work is a meaningful differentiator for analyst candidates because almost no one has one. Real estate finance is one of the last holdouts of "send me your Excel model" culture, which means anyone who shows up with a public portfolio of quantitative work immediately signals a different level of seriousness.

**What recruiters actually look for:**

- **Not:** A GitHub full of academic projects and random scripts
- **Yes:** A focused repository of CRE-specific work with clear business context for each project

**The five pieces that matter:**

1. **CRE Cash Flow Model (Excel, uploaded as PDF or screenshot):** A clean, professionally formatted property-level cash flow model with assumptions clearly labeled, NOI build, and debt sizing output. This is your core analytical credential.

2. **Python Deal Pipeline Tracker (working script):** Even a simple pandas-based deal tracking system signals data engineering capability. Include a README that says "built to solve [specific problem] in a Capital Markets workflow."

3. **Lender Quote Comparison Tool (Python or Excel):** A tool that takes loan parameters as input and outputs a ranked comparison across lender types. This is immediately recognizable as useful to any Capital Markets professional.

4. **Market Comp Analysis (Jupyter Notebook or Excel):** A cap rate / rent analysis using publicly available data (LoopNet, CoStar free tier, CBRE research reports). Show that you can gather market data and extract insight from it.

5. **Sample OM Section (PDF):** A one-page Investment Highlights section for a hypothetical or lightly anonymized deal. This proves writing ability in the Capital Markets context — which Excel projects don't demonstrate.

**What your answer unlocks:**

- If you already have a GitHub profile: Add a CRE-specific repository now. The first push can be incomplete — populate it over 30 days.
- If you don't: Create one today. Repository name: `cre-capital-markets-analyst`. This is not vanity — it is a searchable credential.

**The recruiter screening reality:** Many Capital Markets firms at the analyst level do not have a formal technical screen. But when a recruiter or hiring manager Googles your name and finds a GitHub with CRE-specific analytical work, the first 30 seconds of your interview are already won. You've proven capability before the conversation starts.

## Next Level — Three Milestones That Define Your Trajectory

---

### Milestone 1: Build Your First Complete CRE Deal Model

**Definition of "complete":** A model that a Capital Markets analyst could show a senior broker without embarrassment.

**Minimum viable version:**
- Rent Roll tab: unit mix, SF, in-place rents, lease terms
- Income tab: GPR → vacancy → EGI → expense line items → NOI
- Debt Sizing tab: max loan at 3 DSCR thresholds, LTV check, debt yield check
- Sensitivity tab: 5x5 table with NOI scenarios vs. DSCR thresholds
- Summary tab: 1-page deal snapshot with key metrics

**Timeline:** 5–8 hours of focused work. Do it before you apply anywhere.

**How you'll know it's done:** You can open it in an interview, walk someone through it in 3 minutes, and answer "why did you structure it this way?" without hesitation.

---

### Milestone 2: Write a Perfect Offering Memorandum

**Definition of "perfect":** An OM Investment Highlights section that a managing director at a Capital Markets brokerage could send to a lender without editing.

**The standard:**
- 4–5 bullet points, each a complete declarative sentence
- Every claim backed by a specific number
- Lead bullet: the single most compelling thing about this deal
- No hedging language ("may provide," "could offer") — every sentence makes an assertion
- Ends with the specific loan ask (amount, LTV, DSCR, structure)

**Timeline:** Write your first draft in 60 minutes. Expect to revise it 3–4 times. The gap between your first draft and your fourth draft is where you learn the most.

**How you'll know it's done:** Read it aloud. If every sentence sounds like something a senior broker would actually say, it's done. If any sentence sounds like it came from a textbook, rewrite it.

---

### Milestone 3: Create a Mock Interview + Recruiter Trap Questions

**Definition:** A self-administered practice interview that covers the 10 most common Capital Markets analyst questions, including the 3 trap questions that reveal whether someone actually understands deals.

**The 10 core questions to practice:**
1. Walk me through how you'd underwrite a 100-unit multifamily deal
2. What's the difference between cap rate and IRR — and when does each matter?
3. How do you size a loan when DSCR and LTV give you different maximum amounts?
4. What's a debt yield, and why did lenders start using it after 2010?
5. Walk me through the sections of an offering memorandum
6. What's your current view on cap rates in [specific asset type/market]?
7. Tell me about a deal you've analyzed — what was your recommendation?
8. What's a rent roll, and what do you look for when you first review one?
9. How does a debt fund differ from a bank in terms of what they'll lend on?
10. What would make you pass on a deal that looks good on paper?

**The 3 trap questions (most candidates fail these):**
1. *"What's the current 10-year Treasury rate, and where do you expect spreads to settle for multifamily debt?"* — Tests whether you're tracking actual market conditions
2. *"If you have a deal with 1.18x DSCR and the lender requires 1.25x, name three things you can do to fix it without changing the purchase price"* — Tests whether you understand the real levers
3. *"Tell me what the cap rate means to a lender vs. what it means to a buyer — are they looking at the same number the same way?"* — Tests whether you understand the deal from both sides of the table

**How you'll know you're ready:** Record yourself answering each question. Watch the recording. If you wouldn't hire yourself based on what you see, you're not ready yet.

### What Recruiters Look For in CRE Analyst Candidates

Based on the current hiring market at mid-market and institutional Capital Markets firms:

**Tier 1 signals (open doors):**
- Excel modeling competency (demonstrable, not just claimed)
- Quantitative graduate degree or CFA progress
- Knowledge of current market conditions (rates, spreads, cap rates)
- Commercial lending or corporate finance background

**Tier 2 signals (differentiate within the pool):**
- Written communication ability (ask them to write an Investment Highlights section in the interview)
- Python/data automation skills (almost no analyst candidates have this)
- Market-specific knowledge (can you speak to NYC, Sun Belt, or another specific market with real data?)
- Deal simulation experience (have you actually run a deal from start to finish?)

**Deal-breakers (immediate passes):**
- Vague answers to "walk me through a deal you've analyzed"
- Confusing asset returns (IRR/CoC) with lender metrics (DSCR/Debt Yield)
- Not knowing current market cap rates for the target asset type
- Claiming experience you clearly don't have under 2 minutes of follow-up questions

### LinkedIn Positioning Strategy

**Headline formula:**
> [Credential] | [CRE-relevant skill] + [CRE-relevant skill] | Targeting [specific role]

**Example:**
> M.S. Accounting Analytics | Financial Modeling + Commercial Lending | Targeting CRE Capital Markets Analyst Roles

**About section structure:**
1. Opening line: your core credential in 1 sentence
2. The bridge: how your corporate finance background maps to CRE
3. What you're building: specific CRE skills in progress
4. The ask: open to conversations about Capital Markets opportunities

**Activity strategy:**
- Comment on deal announcements from target firms (CBRE, JLL, Newmark, Walker & Dunlop) with intelligent observations — not praise, but market context
- Share 1–2 posts per month about market conditions using real data
- Follow and engage with Capital Markets professionals at your target firms

**The outreach template:**
> "[Name], I've been following [Firm]'s [asset type] deal volume in [market] — the [specific deal or trend you've noticed] is interesting given [specific market context]. I'm transitioning from corporate finance/commercial lending into CRE Capital Markets and would value 15 minutes to hear your perspective on the market. Happy to connect."

The specificity signals that you've done the research. The ask is small (15 minutes). The tone is peer-level, not supplicant. This gets responses.
