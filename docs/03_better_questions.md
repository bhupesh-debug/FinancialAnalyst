# 5 Ways to Ask Better Questions — Top 1% Style

> **The principle:** In Capital Markets, the quality of your question signals the depth of your thinking more than the answer ever will. Asking "what is DSCR?" tells an interviewer you've read a textbook. Asking "how do lenders balance DSCR against debt yield when those two constraints conflict?" tells them you've thought about how deals actually get done.

This document is not about being clever — it's about asking questions that reveal professional judgment, which is the single most valued signal in Capital Markets hiring.

---

## The 5 Upgrade Frameworks

---

### 1. Ask for Decision Frameworks, Not Definitions

**The mental shift:** Move from "what is X?" to "how do practitioners choose between X and Y when they conflict?"

Definitions live in textbooks. Decision frameworks live in deal experience. When you ask for a decision framework, you're asking someone to share their professional judgment — and that is always a more interesting, more useful conversation.

---

**Bad Question:**
> "Explain DSCR."

This question signals you need orientation. It will get you a textbook definition. You will learn nothing about how deals actually work.

**Better Question:**
> "How do lenders prioritize DSCR versus LTV when deciding loan size — and does that hierarchy change by asset type or market cycle?"

This question signals you understand that DSCR and LTV are both real constraints that can conflict, and that lenders have to choose which one to let bind. The answer will teach you how lenders actually think — which is the information you need in an interview and on the job.

---

**Why This Matters in Capital Markets:**

Every deal involves competing constraints. The analyst's job is not to know the definition of each metric — it's to understand which metric is the binding constraint on a given deal, why, and what the borrower's best path is when two constraints conflict.

In an interview: "Debt yield is 8.5% on this deal, but we need 75% LTV to satisfy the borrower — how would you reconcile that for a CMBS lender?" is an actual interview question. If you've been asking definition questions, you won't have built the decision framework to answer it.

---

**3 Additional Questions in This Category:**

1. *"When a deal fails DSCR but passes LTV, what are the two or three structures a senior broker typically reaches for first — and what signals tell them which one to use?"*

2. *"How does a lender's credit committee weigh sponsor quality against deal quality when the asset is strong but the borrower is new to the asset type?"*

3. *"In a rising rate environment, do lenders protect themselves primarily through DSCR floors, debt yield minimums, or recourse requirements — and is there an order of preference?"*

---

### 2. Ask for Real-World Constraints, Not Idealized Scenarios

**The mental shift:** Move from "how does X work in theory?" to "where does X break in practice?"

Every model is a simplification. Every simplified model has failure modes. Professionals who have run real deals know where models break — and that knowledge is worth more than any textbook description of how models are supposed to work.

---

**Bad Question:**
> "How do you build a CRE cash flow model?"

**Better Question:**
> "Where do CRE models usually break in live deals versus what the Excel assumptions suggest — and which failure modes do lenders flag most often?"

---

**Why This Matters in Capital Markets:**

A CRE deal model in Excel is pristine. A real deal is messy. The rent roll has concessions buried in the comments column. The T-12 has a one-time insurance reimbursement that inflated NOI by $80K. The vacancy assumption is based on the seller's in-place leases, which are all expiring in 14 months.

Lenders know these failure modes. They've seen hundreds of models with the same optimistic assumptions. When you ask about where models break, you're showing that you understand the gap between the model and reality — which is the most important gap a Capital Markets analyst needs to understand.

**3 Additional Questions in This Category:**

1. *"What's the most common mistake first-year analysts make when underwriting multifamily value-add deals — the assumption that looks fine in Excel but kills the deal in due diligence?"*

2. *"When a seller provides a rent roll, what are the first three things an experienced analyst checks before trusting any number in it?"*

3. *"How often do deals close at the terms originally underwritten — and what are the most common sources of retrade between term sheet and closing?"*

---

### 3. Ask for Trade-Offs, Not Rules

**The mental shift:** Move from "what is the rule?" to "what breaks if I change this assumption, and how does the other side of the deal adjust?"

Capital Markets is a negotiation between what the borrower wants and what the lender will accept. Every assumption in a deal model is a potential negotiating point. Understanding trade-offs means understanding what you can give up to get what you need — which is how deals get closed.

---

**Bad Question:**
> "What's a normal rent growth assumption for multifamily?"

**Better Question:**
> "If rent growth is aggressive in the pro forma — say, 4% annually in a market that's been growing at 2% — how do lenders adjust their cap rate or DSCR expectations to protect themselves, and does that adjustment make the deal work or break it?"

---

**Why This Matters in Capital Markets:**

When a deal is borderline — meaning it works at a 1.25x DSCR but not at 1.30x — the analyst's job is to find the set of assumption changes that makes the deal work without being dishonest. Can you justify a slightly lower cap rate with specific comp data? Can you compress OpEx with verifiable operating improvements? Can you restructure the loan term to change the debt constant?

These are trade-off questions. They don't have textbook answers — they have deal-specific answers that require you to understand which variables move together and in which direction.

**3 Additional Questions in This Category:**

1. *"When a borrower pushes for higher proceeds and the only way to get there is to go below a 1.20x DSCR, what structures do debt funds typically use to bridge that gap — and what do they cost in terms of rate or recourse?"*

2. *"If you're 10 basis points too tight on cap rate for the deal to work, what's the fastest legitimate lever to pull — and which ones do lenders accept without pushback vs. which ones trigger a red flag?"*

3. *"How does extending loan term from 5 to 10 years change the risk profile of the deal for both the borrower and the lender — and which party typically benefits more in a normal rate environment?"*

---

### 4. Ask for Output, Not Theory

**The mental shift:** Move from "explain how X works" to "show me a completed X that I could actually use."

Theory without application is almost useless in Capital Markets. Showing someone a formula is not the same as showing them a model. Describing a lender matrix is not the same as building one. When you ask for output, you force the conversation into the territory of execution — which is where Capital Markets analysts actually live.

---

**Bad Question:**
> "Can you explain the debt sizing formula and how DSCR works in the calculation?"

**Better Question:**
> "Show me a lender-ready debt sizing model — not just the formula — with a rent roll, NOI build, debt sizing at three DSCR thresholds, and a sensitivity table. Format it the way a senior analyst at CBRE or JLL would actually present it to a lender."

---

**Why This Matters in Capital Markets:**

Capital Markets is an output business. Deliverables — models, OMs, lender presentations, deal summaries — are what you're judged on. In an interview, if you can say "I built a model like this — here's the structure," you've demonstrated a completely different level of competency than someone who can explain the theory.

When you ask for output, you also learn the format, the presentation conventions, and the level of precision expected — all of which are invisible in a theoretical explanation.

**3 Additional Questions in This Category:**

1. *"Show me what a complete Investment Highlights section looks like for a value-add multifamily deal — not a template, but an actual draft that a senior broker would be willing to put their name on."*

2. *"Build me a lender comparison matrix for a $20M multifamily deal in a secondary market — include bank, debt fund, and agency, with current market pricing, and flag the recommendation with reasoning."*

3. *"Show me the exact email a Capital Markets analyst would send to a lender submitting a new deal for credit review — subject line, opening, deal summary, ask, and closing."*

---

### 5. Ask for Insider Mistakes, Not Best Practices

**The mental shift:** Move from "what should I do?" to "what do people in my position get wrong, and what does that signal to the people evaluating them?"

Best practices are written down everywhere. The mistakes that cost people deals and jobs are not. When you ask for insider mistakes, you get the information that can't be found in textbooks — and that is always the most valuable information.

---

**Bad Question:**
> "What makes a strong financial analyst candidate?"

**Better Question:**
> "What signals make experienced Capital Markets brokers or hiring managers reject a financial analyst candidate immediately — even if their resume looks strong?"

---

**Why This Matters in Capital Markets:**

Capital Markets hiring is pattern-matching against a mental model of what a strong analyst looks like. Experienced professionals have very specific red flags — things that immediately classify a candidate as "doesn't understand how deals work." If you know what those red flags are, you can actively avoid triggering them.

Common red flags (which you now know to avoid):
- Confusing cap rate (income yield) with IRR (levered return)
- Underwriting to a best-case vacancy assumption without stress-testing
- Not knowing current market cap rates for a specific asset type
- Describing ARGUS as a requirement when the interviewer knows it's just Excel with a GUI
- Talking about financial analysis without anchoring it to a specific deal outcome

**3 Additional Questions in This Category:**

1. *"What's the single most common mistake junior analysts make when presenting deal models to senior brokers for the first time — the thing that immediately signals they haven't done this before?"*

2. *"What do experienced lenders or brokers hear in a candidate's language that makes them think 'this person has never closed a real deal'?"*

3. *"In your experience, what separates the analysts who make it past their first year in Capital Markets from the ones who wash out — and is it usually a skill gap or a judgment gap?"*

## Questions That Get You Noticed in Interviews

These five questions demonstrate insider thinking — they show you understand how Capital Markets actually works, not just what the textbooks say. Ask at least two of these at the end of any Capital Markets interview.

---

**Question 1: The Lender Appetite Question**
> "Given where rates and spreads are right now, which lender type is most competitive for [asset type the firm focuses on] — and has that shifted meaningfully from where it was 12–18 months ago?"

*Why this works:* It shows you know that lender competitiveness is dynamic, that market cycles matter, and that you've been paying attention to capital markets conditions. It also invites the interviewer to share deal experience, which is how you build rapport with practitioners.

---

**Question 2: The Binding Constraint Question**
> "On your most recent closing, what was the binding constraint that shaped the final loan structure — DSCR, LTV, or debt yield — and was that what you expected going in?"

*Why this works:* This question can only come from someone who understands how deal constraints work. The phrase "binding constraint" is analyst language that signals quantitative fluency. The interviewer will be surprised by this question and will respect it.

---

**Question 3: The Market Intelligence Question**
> "What's your read on cap rate movement in the [specific submarket or asset type] over the next 6–12 months — are you seeing any divergence between what buyers are underwriting and what lenders are comfortable with?"

*Why this works:* It shows you understand the cap rate / lender appetite relationship, that buyers and lenders can have divergent views, and that you're interested in the market as a practitioner, not just as a student.

---

**Question 4: The Deal Velocity Question**
> "How has deal volume changed for you in the last 12 months, and what's the primary reason — is it rate uncertainty, buyer/seller spread, or something else specific to the asset types you focus on?"

*Why this works:* This is a sophisticated market question that can only come from someone who understands the deal production environment. It also opens a conversation about the firm's recent deal activity, which gives you real intelligence about what you'd be working on.

---

**Question 5: The Analyst Leverage Question**
> "At this firm, what does a first-year analyst typically own end-to-end on a deal — versus what stays with the senior broker — and how does that ownership expand as you develop?"

*Why this works:* This question signals that you're thinking about your development trajectory and contribution, not just about getting the job. It also gives you critical information about what the role actually looks like day-to-day — which is what you need to know to decide if this is the right firm.
