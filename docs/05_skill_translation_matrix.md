### 🔵 BLUE Skills (Full Competency)

#### Financial Modeling (Excel)
**Current level:** Can build dynamic multi-tab workbooks with scenario toggles, linked schedules, and formula-driven outputs from scratch.

**CRE Equivalent:** Property-level cash flow model — the single most important technical deliverable in Capital Markets.

**Gap Severity:** None. The modeling skill is identical; only the income statement structure differs (revenue → GPR, EBIT → NOI).

**Interview Translation:**
> *"I build multi-scenario financial models in Excel from a blank sheet — dynamic assumptions, linked income statements, sensitivity tables. In CRE terms, that directly maps to property-level cash flow modeling: Gross Potential Rent → vacancy assumptions → EGI → operating expenses → NOI, with a debt sizing module layered on top. I've been applying that skill specifically to CRE deal structures."*

---

#### Variance Analysis
**Current level:** Comparing actuals to budget/forecast, isolating variances by driver, writing management commentary.

**CRE Equivalent:** NOI variance analysis — the core discipline of CRE asset management.

**Gap Severity:** None. The analytical methodology is identical.

**Interview Translation:**
> *"In corporate finance, I decompose variances into volume, price, and mix drivers. In CRE, the same logic applies: if actual NOI is below underwriting, I'm decomposing the gap into occupancy variance, revenue variance (loss-to-lease), and expense variance. Same three-step methodology: identify the gap, isolate the driver, quantify the impact."*

---

#### KPI Reporting
**Current level:** Selecting, tracking, and presenting key performance indicators across clients or business units.

**CRE Equivalent:** Deal performance metrics — Cap Rate, DSCR, LTV, Debt Yield.

**Gap Severity:** None for the reporting discipline. Low for the specific CRE KPI definitions.

**Interview Translation:**
> *"I have deep experience structuring KPI frameworks for executive reporting. In CRE Capital Markets, those KPIs are Cap Rate, DSCR, LTV, and Debt Yield. I understand the formulas, the benchmarks, and — critically — how each metric tells a different part of the same deal story: Cap Rate tells you what the market thinks the income stream is worth; DSCR tells you whether the property can service the debt; LTV tells the lender how protected they are; Debt Yield tells the lender their yield independent of cap rate assumptions."*

#### CRE Debt Sizing
**Current level:** Familiar with the conceptual framework (DSCR, LTV) but haven't run the full debt sizing workflow on real CRE deals.

**CRE Equivalent:** Structuring loan terms around property cash flow — the core technical skill of a Capital Markets analyst.

**Gap Severity:** Medium. This is learnable in 2 weeks with focused practice, but it requires running real deals — not just understanding the formula.

**Bridge Strategy:**
1. Memorize the debt sizing formula: `Max Loan = NOI / (DSCR × Debt Constant)`
2. Memorize the debt constant formula: `Debt Constant = Annual P&I / Loan Amount` (varies by rate and amortization)
3. Build a debt sizing calculator in Excel with 3 constraint checks (DSCR, LTV, Debt Yield)
4. Run 5 sample deals through it with different asset types and lender types

**Time to Close:** 2 weeks

**Resource:** MBA (Mortgage Bankers Association) Commercial Real Estate Finance Primer; Trepp Market Data (trepp.com, free research section); Commercial Observer (news + deal data)

**Interview Translation:**
> *"I've built a CRE debt sizing model from scratch — it runs NOI through three constraint checks simultaneously: DSCR floor, LTV ceiling, and debt yield minimum. The binding constraint determines max proceeds. I've run this model on 5+ sample deals and can explain how the max loan changes when you move from a bank to a debt fund to agency lender."*

#### Rent Roll / Lease Analysis
**Current level:** No direct experience reading or modeling rent rolls.

**CRE Equivalent:** Converting a property's lease-level data into a clean, lender-ready cash flow story.

**Gap Severity:** Medium. The data skills to do this exist (Excel, Python, SQL) — the CRE-specific knowledge needed is understanding what questions to ask of the data.

**Bridge Strategy:**
1. Download 2–3 sample rent roll templates (CBRE, JLL, LoopNet)
2. Practice building a GPR schedule from a raw rent roll: unit type → SF → in-place rent → lease expiry → market rent at rollover
3. Identify and flag: above-market leases, below-market leases, near-term expirations, single-tenant concentration risk
4. Model rollover: when each lease expires, what does the rent re-set to? What's the occupancy assumption during the turnover period?

**Time to Close:** 1–2 weeks

**Resource:** BiggerPockets commercial real estate forum (free rent roll templates); CBRE education series

**Interview Translation:**
> *"I've worked with rent roll data specifically — cleaning it, modeling the lease-by-lease cash flow schedule, identifying above and below-market leases, and building the rollover model. My data analysis background (Excel + Python) means I can take a messy rent roll — which every real deal has — and convert it into a clean, lender-ready cash flow story efficiently."*

#### Appraisal Review
**Current level:** No direct CRE appraisal experience.

**CRE Equivalent:** Critically evaluating the assumptions in a third-party appraisal — cap rate, rent growth, vacancy, comparable selection.

**Gap Severity:** Low-Medium. The analytical framework (critical assumption evaluation) is the same as your variance analysis and budget review work. The CRE-specific knowledge is understanding what the "right" assumptions are and how to challenge them.

**Bridge Strategy:**
1. Download 2–3 MAI-format sample appraisals (available from AI online or academic sources)
2. Practice asking: Is the cap rate supported by closed comps? Are the rent growth assumptions consistent with current market data? Did the appraiser use truly comparable properties?
3. Write a one-page critical review of one sample appraisal

**Time to Close:** 1–2 weeks

**Resource:** Appraisal Institute (appraisalinstitute.org) sample reports; CBRE Cap Rate Survey for benchmarking

**Interview Translation:**
> *"Appraisal review is fundamentally critical analysis — you're evaluating whether the assumptions hold up under scrutiny. My variance analysis background gives me that instinct: where is this model most likely wrong, and why? In CRE, that means checking the cap rate against recent closed comps (not listings), stress-testing the rent growth assumption against market absorption data, and verifying the comparable selection methodology."*

## Interview Translation Guide — Talking About Every Skill in CRE Terms

This section gives you the specific scripted language for the most common interview question variations. These are not generic answers — they are constructed to signal CRE-specific thinking, not just corporate finance competency.

---

**"Tell me about your financial modeling experience."**
> *"I build multi-scenario financial models in Excel from scratch — dynamic assumption inputs, linked schedules, sensitivity tables. I've applied this specifically to CRE deal structures: property-level cash flow models (GPR → EGI → NOI), debt sizing at multiple DSCR thresholds, and exit value scenarios based on exit cap rate assumptions. I can build a deal model from a blank sheet in roughly 90 minutes for a standard multifamily deal."*

---

**"Do you have real estate experience?"**
> *"My background is in commercial lending and corporate financial analysis, which I've specifically translated into CRE Capital Markets over the past 30 days. I've run 10+ deal models on live properties — multifamily, industrial — using current market data. I've drafted OM sections, built lender comparison matrices, and studied the debt structures used by banks, debt funds, and agency lenders. I don't have a prior title with 'real estate' in it, but the analytical work I've done is directly applicable."*

---

**"What do you know about DSCR?"**
> *"DSCR is the primary coverage metric a lender uses to size a loan — it's NOI divided by annual debt service. If a lender requires a 1.25x DSCR and NOI is $1.2M, the max debt service is $960K. Combined with the debt constant, that gives you the maximum loan amount. The interesting question is when DSCR and LTV give you different maximums — and the answer is that the loan amount is constrained by whichever is binding. Debt funds typically bind on debt yield rather than DSCR or LTV, which changes the analysis entirely."*

---

**"What's your experience with lenders?"**
> *"In commercial lending at Genpact, I analyzed creditworthiness, structured loan terms, and evaluated amortization schedules across a commercial loan portfolio. In CRE, I've mapped that experience to the three main lender types — banks, debt funds, and agency lenders — and understand how each type approaches the same deal differently: bank lends to the relationship and restricts LTV; debt fund lends to yield and tolerates higher LTV; agency lends to the asset class and offers the lowest rate on qualifying multifamily deals."*

---

**"Why Capital Markets specifically — why not acquisitions or asset management?"**
> *"Capital Markets sits at the intersection of deal analysis and relationship management — you're solving a real financial problem (how do you capitalize this asset optimally?) while managing relationships on both sides of the transaction. My background combines financial modeling precision with client-facing communication, and Capital Markets is the role that requires both. I'm also drawn to the deal velocity — you see more transactions in a year in Capital Markets than you would in three years on the acquisitions side."*
