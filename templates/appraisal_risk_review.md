# APPRAISAL & RISK REVIEW FRAMEWORK
## CRE Capital Markets — Critical Evaluation Checklist

**Document Purpose:** This framework guides analysts through a structured, critical review of a CRE appraisal. Use this checklist every time an appraisal is received on a deal. The goal is to identify risks, validate assumptions, and develop negotiation leverage before presenting findings to the senior broker and lender.

**When to use this document:**
- On receipt of any appraisal from an MAI-certified appraiser
- When reviewing a Broker Price Opinion (BPO) used to underwrite a deal
- When stress-testing sponsor underwriting vs. independent appraisal
- When preparing to challenge an appraisal that is materially lower than expected

**Analyst note:** Appraisers are paid to form an opinion of value — not to be right. Their assumptions are estimates, and they are frequently wrong about vacancy, rent growth, cap rates, and comp selection. Your job is to identify where the assumptions are most vulnerable to challenge — in both directions. An appraiser can overvalue a property (lender risk) or undervalue it (borrower risk). Know which situation you're in.

## SECTION 1: VALUE CONCLUSION REVIEW

> **Objective:** Does the appraised value make sense given the market? Is it supported by independent data, or does it appear to be engineered toward a target value?

### 1.1 Cap Rate Reasonableness Check

The going-in cap rate implied by the appraisal is the most critical single metric. It drives more of the value conclusion than any other input.

| Item | Appraiser Used | Market Range | Analyst Assessment |
|------|--------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Going-In Cap Rate (Income Approach)** | [X.XX]% | [X.X–X.X]% (per comps) | [✓ Within range / ⚠ Below market — investigate / ✗ Unsupported] |
| **Terminal / Reversion Cap Rate** | [X.XX]% | [X.X–X.X]% typical spread of 25–50bps over going-in | [Analyst note] |
| **Discount Rate (DCF)** | [X.X]% | [X.X–X.X]% typical for this property type/market | [Analyst note] |

**Rules of thumb:**
- Going-in cap should be supported by at least 3 closed sales comparables within 12–18 months
- Terminal cap rate should be 25–75 bps higher than going-in (value degradation over time) — anything less is aggressive
- Discount rate should exceed cap rate by 150–300 bps (the required risk premium for illiquidity)
- If cap rate is lower than any comparable sale, demand justification

## SECTION 2: INCOME APPROACH REVIEW

> **Objective:** Scrutinize every assumption in the income approach — this is where appraisers make their most influential and most challengeable judgments. Validate each input against primary market data.

### 2.1 Rent Roll Accuracy

> Compare the appraiser's rent roll to the actual signed leases and current property management system export. Discrepancies are common.

| Check Item | Appraiser Figure | Actual (from Leases/PMS) | Variance | Risk Direction |
|------------|-----------------|--------------------------|---------|---------------|
| Number of units/suites | [XX] | [XX] | [+/-X] | |
| Total NRA/leasable SF | [XX,XXX SF] | [XX,XXX SF] | [+/-X%] | |
| In-place occupied units | [XX] | [XX] | | |
| Gross Potential Rent (GPR) | $[XXX,XXX/yr] | $[XXX,XXX/yr] | [+/-X%] | |
| Market Rent Used | $[X,XXX/unit or $XX.XX/SF] | $[X,XXX / $XX.XX] — per your comps | [+/-X%] | |
| Lease expiration dates | [Match leases?] | Verify against rent roll | | |

**Common appraiser errors to check:**
- [ ] Using asking rents instead of effective (net of concessions) rents
- [ ] Ignoring loss-to-lease on below-market units
- [ ] Excluding parking, laundry, or ancillary income
- [ ] Counting physically vacant units as occupied
- [ ] Using stabilized (future) rent on a currently below-market unit
- [ ] Applying the wrong lease type (NNN vs. gross) to expense reimbursement math

**Rules of thumb for vacancy:**
- Multifamily (stabilized, good market): 3–5% vacancy standard; <3% is aggressive; >8% suggests distress
- Class A Office: 10–15% vacancy standard; sub-10% is aggressive for most markets currently
- Industrial (strong market): 3–5% standard; many appraisers appropriately use 3–5%
- Retail (neighborhood/community): 5–10% standard; varies dramatically by tenancy and market
- Anything below 3% for any asset class requires specific market data justification

## SECTION 3: SALES COMPARISON REVIEW

> **Objective:** Assess whether the sales comparables selected by the appraiser are truly comparable and whether adjustments are appropriate and well-supported.

### 3.1 Comparable Selection Quality

For each comp used by the appraiser, evaluate:

| Comp | Address | Sale Date | Sale Price | Price/Unit or SF | Cap Rate | Distance | Age | Size vs. Subject | Adjustment | Appropriate? |
|------|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|---------|---------|-----|-----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Comp 1 | [Address] | [DATE] | $[XX,XXX,XXX] | $[XXX,XXX] | [X.X]% | [X.X mi] | [YEAR] | [XX% larger/smaller] | [+/-X%] | [Y / N — explain] |
| Comp 2 | [Address] | [DATE] | $[XX,XXX,XXX] | $[XXX,XXX] | [X.X]% | [X.X mi] | [YEAR] | [XX% larger/smaller] | [+/-X%] | [Y / N — explain] |
| Comp 3 | [Address] | [DATE] | $[XX,XXX,XXX] | $[XXX,XXX] | [X.X]% | [X.X mi] | [YEAR] | [XX% larger/smaller] | [+/-X%] | [Y / N — explain] |

**Comparable selection red flags:**
- [ ] Comps are more than 18–24 months old in an active market (stale data)
- [ ] All comps are superior to the subject (will require all negative adjustments → likely overvalue)
- [ ] All comps are inferior to the subject (will require all positive adjustments → likely undervalue)
- [ ] Comps are in different submarkets without explicit market adjustment
- [ ] Cherry-picked comps that exclude recent lower-priced sales nearby
- [ ] Comps in different property class without class adjustment
- [ ] Outlier comps not excluded or disclosed

- Total net adjustment to any single comp should not exceed 25% — anything larger suggests the comp is not truly comparable

## SECTION 4: MARKET ASSUMPTIONS REVIEW

> **Objective:** Validate the forward-looking assumptions that drive value in the income approach DCF. These are the most subjective inputs and the most frequently manipulated.

### 4.1 Rent Growth Rate Analysis

| Assumption | Appraiser Used | Market Evidence | Assessment |
|------------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| Year 1 Rent Growth | [X.X]% | [Submarket T-12: X.X% per CoStar] | [Reasonable / Aggressive / Conservative] |
| Year 2–5 Rent Growth (avg) | [X.X]% | [Long-run avg for market: X.X%] | |
| Year 6–10 Rent Growth (avg) | [X.X]% | [GDP proxy: 2–3% / inflation-linked] | |
| Terminal Growth Rate | [X.X]% | [Standard: 1–3%; >3% is aggressive] | |

**Red flags:**
- Year 1 rent growth that is meaningfully above T-12 actual growth without explanation
- Long-run growth above 3–4% annually — this is above GDP growth and requires extraordinary justification
- Rent growth rate that exactly equals the broker's assumptions (appraiser may have relied on broker OM)

### 4.2 Expense Growth Assumptions

| Assumption | Appraiser Used | Benchmark | Assessment |
|------------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Expense Growth Rate (annual) | [X.X]% | [CPI / 2–3% standard] | |
| Tax Reassessment Modeled? | [Y/N] | Required at acquisition or reassessment trigger | |
| Insurance Cost Trend | [X.X]% | [5–15% recent market — many appraisers use 3%] | |

**Note:** Insurance costs have increased dramatically in coastal/CAT-exposed markets (FL, TX Gulf, CA earthquake zones, Southeast Atlantic). If appraiser is using a 2–3% insurance growth rate in a CAT-exposed market, flag as materially understated.

### 4.3 Cap Rate Analysis — Going-In vs. Terminal

| Cap Rate | Appraiser Used | Market Evidence | Assessment |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| Going-In Cap Rate | [X.XX]% | [Supported by sales comp range: X.X–X.X%] | |
| Terminal / Exit Cap Rate | [X.XX]% | [Should be 25–75bps above going-in, typically] | |
| Spread (Terminal − Going-In) | [+X]bps | [Market standard: +25–75bps] | |

**Red flag:** If terminal cap rate ≤ going-in cap rate, the appraiser is assuming the property becomes more valuable over the hold period purely through cap rate compression — which is speculative in most market conditions.

**Stress test:** What does value look like at terminal cap +50bps? +100bps? If loan proceeds are sensitive to a 50bps cap rate shift, note this as a risk.

## SECTION 6: RECOMMENDED ACTIONS

### 6.1 Scenarios Requiring Re-Appraisal

Recommend requesting a revised appraisal or challenging the existing appraisal if:
- [ ] Appraised value is more than 15% above or below the median of sales comparables (without compelling market-specific justification)
- [ ] Appraiser used comps older than 18 months in an active/changing market without a time adjustment
- [ ] Appraiser's stabilized NOI is more than 15% above T-12 actuals without a documented lease-up or rent growth rationale
- [ ] Comparable selection appears cherry-picked (all comps superior or all comps inferior)
- [ ] Expense ratio is below 30% for a full-service property without RUBS or NNN income
- [ ] Terminal cap rate is below the going-in cap rate
- [ ] Property has changed materially since the effective date (major tenant departure, casualty, new construction nearby)
- [ ] DSCR at the appraised value supports a materially different loan amount than the deal requires

**Process for challenging an appraisal:**
1. Document all specific objections in writing with supporting market data
2. Request a meeting with the appraiser to discuss — do not send objections to the lender first
3. Provide new or overlooked comparables with descriptions
4. If appraiser declines to revise, present objections to lender with a position paper
5. Lender may order an appraisal review (desk review or field review) from a second MAI appraiser

### 6.2 Negotiation Leverage Points

Use findings from this checklist to negotiate with: (a) the borrower on terms or deal structure, (b) the appraiser on value, or (c) the lender on underwriting.

| Finding | Leverage With | Action |
|---------|--------------|--------|
| Appraiser NOI [X]% below underwriting | Borrower | Adjust OM underwriting to match appraisal expectation; address specific variance items |
| Appraiser used above-market cap rate | Borrower / Appraiser | Provide 3 recent sales comps at tighter cap rates; request appraiser review |
| Expense ratio too high (appraiser added management overhead that doesn't exist) | Appraiser / Lender | Provide actual management agreement; document self-managed status |
| Deferred maintenance identified | Borrower | Negotiate CapEx escrow funded at close to satisfy lender requirements |
| Tenant credit quality weaker than presented | Lender (proactively) | Get ahead of issue; provide tenant financials and payment history |
| Environmental REC identified | Borrower / Lender | Require Phase II; cost estimate for remediation; indemnity |
| Rent roll discrepancy (appraisal vs. actual) | Appraiser | Provide current rent roll with executed lease documentation |

---

### 6.3 Items to Discuss with Borrower

Before presenting the appraisal analysis to the lender, confirm the following with the borrower:

- [ ] Does the appraised value support the requested loan amount at the target LTV? If not, what is the revised loan amount?
- [ ] Are there factual errors in the appraisal (wrong SF, wrong unit count, incorrect lease dates) that require correction?
- [ ] Are all environmental conditions disclosed to the lender? (Non-disclosure can trigger fraud carve-outs on non-recourse loans)
- [ ] Are there any pending lease negotiations or tenant issues not reflected in the appraisal?
- [ ] Are CapEx needs identified by the PCA/appraisal budgeted and acceptable?
- [ ] Does the borrower agree with the appraiser's stabilized value, or do they wish to contest specific assumptions?
